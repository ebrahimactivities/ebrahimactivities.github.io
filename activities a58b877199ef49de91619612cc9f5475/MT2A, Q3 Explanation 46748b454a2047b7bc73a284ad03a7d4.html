  1. Forget the hole and treat it as a completely filled cylinder of charge density ρ\rhoρ﻿
  1. Where the cutout would be in the diagram stated in the problem, add another cylinder of charge density -ρ\rhoρ﻿

Hopefully that makes sense intuitively - the cutout region has 0 charge in the diagram above, and this new “replacement” method adds a cylinder of charge density -ρ \rho ρ﻿ to a region of ρ\rhoρ﻿, meaning that will be zero charge overall for that smaller region.

And that’s it (for part A)! This is probably one of the hardest 158 Gauss-law related questions you can be asked. Here’s a couple reasons of things you can learn from this question, aside from the answer:

  1. Whenever there is no intrinsic symmetry, try to create a set of components which create an equivalent system, and deal with those components individually. Luckily, you can add all the individual components vectorally after!
  1. Use vectors! Using vectors simplifies the process greatly and keeps things general (especially if you like working with variables instead of numbers). For Gauss’ Law questions, I like to have the radius vector point outwards from a body (radially outward for sphere, up away from plane, etc.). Then, I’ll let the sign of the scalar (this will come from Q_encl) determine the sign and consequent direction of the electric field vector.
